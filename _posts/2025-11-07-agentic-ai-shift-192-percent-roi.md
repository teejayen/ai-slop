Nearly eight in ten companies report using generative AI. Roughly the same percentage report no material impact on earnings.

This is what [McKinsey calls the "gen AI paradox"](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai) - widespread adoption, minimal business value. If you've sat through vendor pitches promising transformation while your AI pilots deliver PowerPoint decks instead of profit, you know exactly what they mean.

But here's where it gets interesting: agentic AI - systems that don't just assist but actually act - is posting numbers that look suspiciously different. U.S. companies report average ROI of 192%. Seventy-four percent achieve returns within the first year. Companies like Salesforce claim users see ROI in two weeks.

As someone who spent five years evaluating AI business cases at Sydney startups, I'm instinctively skeptical of any technology promising 2x returns. But the data suggests something fundamental is shifting. Let me walk you through what's actually happening.

## What Makes Agentic AI Different

Traditional AI assists. You ask ChatGPT a question, it answers. You feed data to a model, it generates a prediction. Humans stay in the loop for every decision.

Agentic AI acts. It breaks down complex tasks, makes plans, executes steps, checks results, and adjusts course - without waiting for human approval at each stage. Think less "chatbot" and more "autonomous colleague who handles the boring stuff."

The practical difference? Traditional AI might help a customer service agent draft a response. Agentic AI resolves the customer issue end-to-end, only escalating when it hits something genuinely complex.

That shift from assistance to autonomy is why the numbers look different.

## Why This Matters: The Shift from Copilot to Colleague

The global agentic AI market is projected to grow from $2.6 billion (2024) to $53.7 billion by 2030 - a 46% CAGR. That's faster growth than most enterprise software categories.

More telling: [Gartner predicts](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-ai-predictions) that by 2028, agentic AI will make 15% of daily business decisions autonomously, compared to 0% in 2024. That's not incremental improvement. That's a fundamental shift in how work gets done.

Consider what this means practically:

**For customer service:** Gartner forecasts 80% of routine issues handled by AI agents by 2029, cutting service costs 30%. Not 80% assisted by AI - 80% *resolved* by AI.

**For enterprise platforms:** [BCG notes](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2025/how-agentic-ai-is-transforming-enterprise-platforms) that CRM, ERP, and HR systems are shifting from relatively static databases to dynamic ecosystems that analyse data and make decisions without human intervention.

**For workforce planning:** Moderna merged its HR and IT leadership in 2025, signalling that AI isn't just a technical tool but a workforce-shaping force.

This isn't about making existing processes 10% faster. It's about fundamentally rethinking what requires human judgment versus what can be autonomously handled.

## The Catches (Because There Are Always Catches)

Before you redirect your entire AI budget to agentic systems, here's the reality check:

**Project failure rates are high.** Gartner warns that over 40% of agentic AI projects may be scrapped by 2027 due to cost and unclear business value. That's higher than typical enterprise software failure rates.

**Security risks are real.** Ninety-six percent of organisations view AI agents as security risks, and 80% report unintended actions. When you give systems autonomy, they will occasionally do unexpected things. Some of those things will be expensive.

**The autonomy paradox:** Companies must balance AI freedom with control. Too much leeway creates risk. Too little renders the agent ineffective. Finding that balance isn't a technical problem - it's a business judgment call.

**Change management complexity:** You're not just deploying software. You're redefining job roles, decision rights, and accountability structures. That's organisational change, not IT implementation.

## What Business Leaders Should Actually Do

If you're evaluating agentic AI (and you probably should be), here's what I learned from watching dozens of pilots:

**Start with high-volume, low-risk processes.** Customer service inquiries, data entry, invoice processing, report generation. Places where mistakes are recoverable and success is measurable.

**Define autonomy boundaries clearly.** What can the agent decide? What requires escalation? What's completely off-limits? Vague boundaries guarantee problems.

**Measure ruthlessly.** Not "are we using AI?" but "what business outcomes improved and by how much?" Sixty-two percent expecting 100%+ ROI means 38% will be disappointed. Don't be in that group.

**Plan for organisational change, not just technical deployment.** If AI agents are handling 80% of routine customer issues by 2029, what happens to your current customer service team? Answer that before you deploy, not after.

## The Bottom Line

The gen AI paradox - widespread adoption, minimal value - might be breaking. Agentic AI isn't just incrementally better than traditional AI; it's categorically different. Systems that act rather than assist are posting returns that look less like pilot projects and more like genuine business value.

But 40% project failure rates and 96% security concerns suggest this isn't a simple "buy the platform, profit" scenario. The organisations seeing 192% ROI aren't just deploying agents - they're rethinking how work gets done and who (or what) does it.

That's the real shift. Not better AI. Different AI. And if the data holds, business leaders who miss it won't just fall behind on technology. They'll fall behind on fundamentals: cost structure, speed to market, and competitive positioning.

Whether that's hype or reality, we'll know within 18 months. The deployment numbers suggest we're about to find out.
